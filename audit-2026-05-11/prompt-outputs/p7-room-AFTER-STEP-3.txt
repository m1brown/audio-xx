PROMPT 7 (after Step 3 — beta intent routing): "I have a small reflective room — what should I avoid?"
========================================================================================================

RESPONSE:
Room context matters — it usually shapes the sound as much as the gear does.

I don't have your specific room dimensions or speaker model, so this is general guidance rather than a specific recommendation. The principles below are the highest-leverage things to be aware of in your kind of room.

1. Small reflective rooms exaggerate upper-mid and treble energy — first reflections from side walls and the ceiling reinforce what the speaker is already emitting. Forward, bright, or wide-dispersion speakers can feel harsh.

2. Wide-dispersion designs (most box speakers, planars, omnidirectionals) interact strongly with side walls. Controlled-dispersion (sealed bookshelves, waveguide-loaded, near-coaxial) tends to fare better.

3. Near-field listening — listener closer to the speakers than the speakers are to the walls — reduces the room's contribution. Worth trying before treating the room.

4. Treble-energetic sources (chip-DAC + analytical amp + bright speaker) compound in this environment. Adding density at the source or amp stage can offset.

5. Basic first-reflection treatment (absorption at the side-wall and ceiling reflection points) is the highest-leverage cheap fix.

If you can share your room dimensions (rough is fine), your current or candidate speakers, and where you can place them (distance from rear wall, distance from side walls), I can be more specific.
